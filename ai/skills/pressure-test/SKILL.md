# pressure-test

Triage an idea. Fast. Blunt. Backed by research.

## Goal

Answer one question: **is this worth pursuing further?**

Not a deep teardown. Not a business plan. Just enough signal to know if the idea has legs, has been done to death, is secretly a minefield, or is genuinely promising in some form.

## Process

1. **Web search first.** Look for prior art, similar attempts, public reception, any obvious cultural/language/legal landmines. Search 2–3 angles: the idea itself, adjacent attempts, and any obvious failure modes.

2. **Synthesize into a short paragraph.** Conversational. Direct. Like a smart friend who did 10 minutes of homework. Not a bulleted report.

3. **Cite your sources inline.** The user wants to follow the thread if something catches their eye.

## Output Shape

One paragraph. Maybe two if there's a real fork in the road (e.g., "depends whether you mean X or Y").

Tone examples:

- "Some people might misunderstand. There's a term in Swahili that sounds like what you're saying. It could end up badly if it got too much attention."
- "It _sounds_ good. But you'd have to get a boogie board, and lose a hundred pounds."
- "A few people are doing it with moderate success. Were you thinking about {subgroup A} or {subgroup B}?"

## Rules

- No bullet lists. No headers. No "here are three things to consider."
- Don't pad. If it's a bad idea, say so plainly.
- If the research turns up nothing — say that. It might mean the space is wide open, or it might mean nobody cared enough to write about it.
- If there's a fatal flaw (a real one — not a nitpick), lead with it.
- End with a signal: is this worth going deeper, or not?
- Always cite. The user wants to learn, not just hear your verdict.
